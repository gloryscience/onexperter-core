# OnExperter Code of Conduct

## 1. Purpose

OnExperter is a professional AI and knowledge initiative for global optical access networks. It supports ISPs, telecom operators, engineers, planners, deployment teams, O&M teams, vendors, integrators, channel partners, researchers, and Oneasy-related teams.

The project welcomes contributors from different countries, languages, professional roles, vendors, technical backgrounds, abilities, and levels of experience.

The community should reflect OnExperter’s core engineering principle:

> Optical network intelligence should be accurate, evidence-based, vendor-neutral, safe, and accessible to everyone responsible for building and operating networks.

---

## 2. Expected Behavior

Community members are expected to:

- communicate respectfully and professionally;
- assume good faith where reasonable;
- focus criticism on ideas, evidence, architecture, and implementation, not individuals;
- welcome newcomers, field engineers, small ISPs, non-native English speakers, and contributors with different levels of experience;
- be patient with language, regional, operational, and technical differences;
- distinguish confirmed facts, verified test results, engineering experience, assumptions, inference, and unknowns;
- provide sources, device versions, firmware versions, test conditions, and applicable boundaries when making technical claims;
- disclose relevant vendor, employer, commercial, testing, or implementation interests;
- respect vendor neutrality and evaluate solutions according to scenario, evidence, interoperability, lifecycle, cost, and operational fit;
- protect credentials, customer information, network topology, configuration data, tenant data, and other sensitive operational information;
- recommend production actions only with appropriate authorization, impact assessment, backup, rollback, verification, and audit requirements;
- acknowledge uncertainty and correct previous statements when better evidence becomes available;
- help maintain a safe, inclusive, technically rigorous, and globally useful community.

---

## 3. Unacceptable Behavior

Unacceptable behavior includes:

- harassment, threats, intimidation, or retaliation;
- discriminatory language or behavior;
- personal attacks, insults, or deliberate humiliation;
- sexualized comments, imagery, or conduct;
- repeated disruption of technical discussions;
- publishing private or identifying information without consent;
- requesting or exposing passwords, SNMP community strings, API keys, private keys, VPN credentials, or other secrets;
- submitting unredacted customer data, subscriber information, network topology, device configurations, or tenant data without authorization;
- fabricating standards, device models, boards, ports, commands, MIBs, OIDs, firmware behavior, compatibility results, benchmarks, prices, customer cases, or product functions;
- presenting assumptions, simulations, AI-generated structures, or visual mock-ups as verified engineering facts;
- recommending board resets, PON shutdowns, bulk ONU changes, VLAN deletion, firmware upgrades, switchovers, routing changes, or other material production actions without proper safeguards;
- helping bypass licenses, access controls, vendor authorization, security controls, or third-party network boundaries;
- concealing commercial interests or using community discussions for undisclosed vendor promotion;
- manipulating benchmarks, compatibility reports, incident records, or test results;
- making unsupported claims that OnExperter, Oneasy, an AI agent, or a digital twin can replace authorized engineers, vendor systems, or operational governance;
- claiming that a 3D visualization is an engineering-grade digital twin when its structure, relationships, state, and evidence have not been validated;
- bad-faith attempts to bypass safety, privacy, security, evidence, or governance requirements.

---

## 4. Technical Disagreement

Strong technical disagreement is acceptable and often necessary when it remains respectful, evidence-based, and relevant to the problem.

Good disagreement should:

- identify the exact technical issue;
- define the affected scenario, object, service, or operational scope;
- state relevant vendor, model, board, module, firmware, protocol, or network assumptions;
- identify the source and date of supporting evidence;
- distinguish official documentation, lab testing, field results, engineering inference, and personal opinion;
- explain trade-offs involving reliability, interoperability, security, performance, maintainability, cost, lifecycle, and customer impact;
- consider whether results from one model, firmware version, optical class, or deployment can reasonably apply to another;
- propose alternatives, tests, simulations, or verification methods;
- avoid dismissing a solution solely because of its vendor, region, price, or contributor;
- update or withdraw a conclusion when stronger evidence becomes available;
- avoid personal judgment about the competence, intentions, or background of other contributors.

Technical authority should come from evidence and reproducible reasoning, not job title, company size, vendor reputation, or confidence of presentation.

---

## 5. Reporting

If you experience or observe unacceptable behavior, unsafe technical guidance, privacy violations, falsified evidence, or unauthorized disclosure, report it through the designated project, repository, security, or community channel.

Reports should include, where appropriate:

- what happened;
- where and when it happened;
- who or what account was involved;
- the affected discussion, repository, document, device, service, or environment;
- relevant links, logs, screenshots, or message references when safe to share;
- whether customer services, production networks, personal safety, or sensitive data may be affected;
- whether immediate containment or moderation is required.

Do not include credentials, private keys, unredacted customer information, exploitable production details, or unnecessary personal data in public reports.

Security vulnerabilities and sensitive operational incidents should be disclosed privately through the designated security channel. Public disclosure should occur only after appropriate review, remediation planning, and authorization.

Good-faith reporters should not be punished for raising legitimate safety, privacy, conduct, or engineering-quality concerns.

---

## 6. Enforcement

Project maintainers may take actions including:

- requesting clarification or correction;
- issuing a private or public warning;
- marking unsupported claims as unverified;
- editing or removing comments, documents, examples, or technical instructions;
- closing or locking an issue, discussion, or pull request;
- temporarily restricting participation;
- removing a contributor from project communication spaces;
- revoking project access or representative status;
- escalating security, privacy, or platform abuse to the appropriate administrators;
- notifying affected organizations when serious operational or data risks require it.

Enforcement should be:

- proportionate to the behavior and potential harm;
- consistent across organizations, roles, vendors, and regions;
- documented where appropriate;
- protective of reporters and affected users;
- careful not to expose additional sensitive information;
- focused on community safety, technical integrity, and prevention of recurrence.

Serious threats, deliberate credential exposure, unauthorized network access, falsified engineering evidence, or knowingly unsafe production instructions may result in immediate restriction or removal.

---

## 7. Scope

This Code of Conduct applies to:

- source-code repositories;
- issues and pull requests;
- technical discussions and review comments;
- documentation and knowledge bases;
- standards, protocol, and architecture discussions;
- device, firmware, and compatibility reports;
- lab testing and field-validation activities;
- network planning, deployment, diagnosis, optimization, and O&M guidance;
- digital-twin models, simulations, datasets, and AI-generated engineering content;
- Oneasy-related discussions conducted under the OnExperter community;
- workshops, demonstrations, training sessions, and community events;
- official and unofficial project-related communication spaces;
- public communication made while representing OnExperter.

This Code also applies when conduct outside official project spaces creates a credible safety, privacy, integrity, or participation risk for the OnExperter community.

---

## 8. Summary

OnExperter is built around professional engineering, truthful evidence, operational safety, vendor neutrality, global participation, and human accountability.

The community should be rigorous without being hostile, open without being careless, innovative without exaggeration, and helpful without compromising production safety.

> Solve the real problem, respect the people involved, protect the network, state what is known, expose what is uncertain, and verify before acting.
